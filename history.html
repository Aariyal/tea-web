History of Tea

A journey through the history and culture of the world's favourite drink.

Every culture around the world has shaped the way it enjoys tea. In Bangladesh, tea has its own story and taste.

A Brief Tea-Time Story

Tea plants are native to East Asia, possibly originating near the Irrawaddy River and spreading to regions such as southeast China, Indo-China, and Assam. Their natural habitat lies between Nagaland, Manipur, and Mizoram, extending east to Zhejiang Province in China and south to Burma, Thailand, and Vietnam. The Chinese small-leaf tea (C. sinensis var. sinensis) may have evolved in southern China, while the Chinese Assam-type tea (C. sinensis var. assamica) has two parentages: one from southern Yunnan and another from western Yunnan, the latter sharing genetic ties with Indian Assam tea. Divergence estimates suggest Chinese small-leaf tea split from Assam tea around 22,000 years ago, correlating with the last glacial maximum, while the divergence between Chinese Assam and Indian Assam teas occurred approximately 2,800 years ago.

Tea has been consumed in East Asia for centuries, initially eaten raw or added to dishes before being brewed. It likely began in Yunnan for medicinal use and evolved into a bitter beverage in Sichuan. Chinese legends credit the mythical Shennong with tea's invention around 2737 BC, although its introduction into China may have come from the southwest. The earliest written records reference tea, with 'tú' appearing in ancient texts. Tea drinking gained prominence during the Han dynasty, evidenced by physical finds from the mausoleum of Emperor Jing, and was first documented in a 59 BC contract. By the Tang dynasty, tea became popular, spreading to Korea, Japan, and Vietnam, with Lu Yu's "The Classic of Tea" detailing its preparation and influencing its cultural integration.

Throughout history, various techniques and forms of tea have evolved. In the Han and Six Dynasties, tea was steamed, pounded, shaped into cakes, and then boiled with flavors like orange peels and mint. The Tang dynasty saw a similar preparation, though Lu Yu criticized additional flavors. By the Song dynasty, loose-leaf tea emerged. During the Yuan and Ming dynasties, unoxidized leaves were pan-stirred and air-dried to maintain their green color. The 15th century introduced oolong tea with partial oxidation, while Western preferences leaned towards fully oxidized black tea. Yellow tea was an accidental creation during the Ming dynasty's green tea production, resulting from careless processing that altered its flavor.

Tea in Bangladesh

Bengal historically served as the terminus of the Tea Horse Road, connecting the Indian subcontinent to China's early tea-growing regions in Yunnan. One of the earliest Bengali tea drinkers was Atisa. The cultivation of black tea in Bengal and Assam began during the British Empire, notably in the Sylhet district of Assam. In 1834, Robert Bruce identified tea plants in the Khasi and Jaintia Hills, leading to the formation of the Assam Tea Company in 1839, which included prominent businessmen such as Haji Mohammed Hashim, Dwarkanath Tagore, and Mutty Lall Seal, and was linked to Calcutta's Bengal Tea Association.

European traders established the first tea gardens in Chittagong in 1840, using Chinese tea plants sourced from the Calcutta Botanical Garden. The first local tea was produced near the Karnaphuli River in Chittagong in 1843, and commercial tea cultivation commenced in Sylhet's Mulnicherra Estate in 1857. The Surma River Valley emerged as the main center for tea cultivation in Eastern Bengal, with plantations in Lower Tippera (modern Comilla) and North Bengal's Panchagarh, the latter being a significant tea zone in Bangladesh.

Tea became a vital export product of British Bengal. The Assam Bengal Railway played a crucial role in the industry by facilitating the transportation of tea from the Surma and Brahmaputra Valleys to exporters at the Port of Chittagong. Notable pioneers of the native tea industry included Syed Abdul Majid, alongside several local entrepreneurs who began establishing their companies in the early twentieth century.

The Chittagong Tea Auction, founded in 1949 by British and Australian traders, marked a significant development in the tea trade. Dominance in the industry was once held by British companies like James Finlay and Duncan Brothers, while the Ispahani family emerged as a significant force in tea production and trade.
